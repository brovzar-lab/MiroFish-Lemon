PILOT SYNOPSIS — 101 "SPOILED ROTTEN"
V8.2

The pilot opens in the study of the Serrano Estate with an iconic image: a golden avocado in a lit display case. The camera pulls back to reveal violent chaos as the body of Isabela Serrano (24) crashes through the glass. A man with his back to us grabs her by the hair. The camera doesn't move. We only see the horror fragmented in the wall mirror. Isabela begs. No one listens. Then silence.

SUPER: 6 DAYS EARLIER.

Benjamín (35) is in a private suite at AT&T Stadium in Dallas during a Cowboys game. He's wearing a team jersey — his costume for the pitch. He's working Jack Emerson (60), a billionaire Texan, trying to sell him on Grupo Serrano's supply chain technology. Emerson isn't buying it. He cuts Benjamín off before he gets to the numbers: technology can be copied, he says. What can't be copied is the name. Benjamín's grandmother built that empire with her presence, her face in the right rooms — no government, no bank, no Walmart was going to question the woman who came from nothing and built it all. Emerson demands thirty million in escrow. Benjamín's phone buzzes with missed calls from Ernesto.

Karla (40) is in her penthouse jacuzzi watching Ingrid Cervantes anchor the evening news when her lover Sophia walks in. Karla kills the TV. A message from Ernesto arrives: their grandmother needs to see them. Isabela boards a private jet alone, nervous, pressing an AA sobriety chip between her fingers.

At the entrance to the estate, Ernesto Vega (64) — the family's lawyer and confessor — steps outside to find Don Ezequiel (65) blocking the driveway, flanked by his men Beto and Reynaldo. Ezequiel complains that Ernesto hasn't been returning his calls. The conversation is measured and controlled. Ezequiel doesn't need to raise his voice. Ernesto assures him everything will proceed as agreed. Ezequiel says he doesn't like silence.

The three siblings reunite at the estate. Benjamín and Karla's dynamic is immediately corrosive. While they wait, Benjamín's eyes land on a framed photo of Carmen on the wall — Carmen at a gala, immaculate, surrounded by politicians and ministers. He studies it for a moment. "She was always so perfect for the cameras," he says, almost to himself. Karla, without turning: "It was her job."

In Doña Carmen Serrano's (81) bedroom, their grandmother receives them with devastating clarity: she tells them she failed them, that she made them useless. She takes a blue folder, signs it with a trembling hand, and announces she is removing them from her will. The blow lands hard. Carmen delivers her verdict with fire in her eyes: "IT'S MY MONEY, BENJAMÍN. MINE." A bloody cough cuts her off.

At a bar, the siblings map out their next move: there's a power vacuum and they're going to fill it. Benjamín will take Carmen's desk at Grupo Serrano headquarters. Karla will go to Ernesto and figure out how to fight the will. Isabela will stay close to Carmen.

At the port of Lázaro Cárdenas, Don Ezequiel listens as Salvador (70) warns him about a wildcat strike set for the following morning. The workers can't take it anymore. Ezequiel slides him a stack of cash to buy some time.

At Grupo Serrano headquarters, Gabriela (70) — Carmen's longtime personal assistant — gives Benjamín the lay of the land: CFO Javier Cordero (40) has been running things like he owns them. Meanwhile, Isabela takes advantage of a moment alone with Carmen to dig through her files. She finds a photograph of a young Carmen standing in fields of red flowers — poppies — with a man she doesn't recognize. On the back, in handwriting: "Tierra Caliente, 1982. CS and EA." She also finds a ledger from the 1980s with figures in the hundreds of millions. The heart monitor spikes. Isabela hides everything.

Benjamín handles a protest that has shut down the packaging plant. It's led by Lucía (35), an activist who accuses Grupo Serrano of poisoning the soil and draining the region's water supply. Benjamín talks her into a formal meeting at seven. Later, Isabela confronts him with what she found — poppies are poppies. Benjamín brushes her off and walks out. Karla visits Ernesto. He lays it out plainly: fifty thousand pesos a month is what they'll receive while they get back on their feet. Karla confronts him with their shared history, all the years he was part of the family. Ernesto opens the safe behind a Remedios Varo painting and places the blue folder inside. He can't help them.

In the conference room, Benjamín offers Lucía a hundred million pesos in community compensation. Javier walks in and dismantles every promise with precise data. Benjamín is left with nothing to say. After Javier leaves, Lucía is packing up her things. She tells Benjamín something he can't quite make sense of: she spent years trying to get a meeting with this company and was ignored every time — but the moment Carmen died, someone called her to offer condolences, like she was family. "The relationships this company has aren't with Grupo Serrano," she says on her way out. "They're with the Serranos. That's not the same thing." She leaves him with a final line: "You know what's worse than doing nothing? Pretending like you care."

In the avocado groves at night, the siblings agree to steal the codicil. Karla has someone who can get into Ernesto's house. Their alibi: Benjamín at the craps table, Karla at a hotel bar, Isabela at a restaurant. The thief breaks into Ernesto's study, cracks the safe, takes the blue folder. Footsteps from upstairs. Ernesto is moving toward the sound.

The next morning, Benjamín arrives at Grupo Serrano to find employees crying in the hallways. Gabriela delivers the news: Ernesto was murdered during a robbery. Benjamín locks himself in the executive bathroom. He calls Karla, who is already with Commander Ruiz from the prosecutor's office, carefully managing her story.

A night of quiet grief settles over the estate. A flashback takes us back: ten-year-old Benjamín arrives at the hacienda smiling, oblivious to the fact that everyone around him is dressed in black. Ernesto is the one who tells him his parents aren't coming home.

Carmen is dying. Doctor Morales arrives with her advance directives: withdraw life support. The siblings say their goodbyes. Isabela talks about frogs that leap forward when they're afraid. Karla promises to protect the family. Benjamín starts with tenderness — then leans close to Carmen's ear and turns the farewell into a declaration of war: "Your little plan to throw us out on the street didn't work."

The memorial at the packaging plant draws more than a thousand people. Javier tries to block Benjamín from the podium. Karla shuts him down. Benjamín's speech is the most calculated performance of the episode: he announces compensation for affected families, symbolically stakes his claim to the leadership of Grupo Serrano, and holds Javier's stare. Then he drops his note cards. He talks about Carmen's hands — working hands, not the hands of a wealthy woman — and about how when he was small and afraid she would squeeze his hand in hers and all the fear would disappear. The crowd is moved. From the back of the plant, Javier watches. His face isn't contempt. It's recognition — he could never do that. Don Ezequiel, watching from his ranch, goes still before exploding. He turns to Beto. "Could you do that?" Beto has no answer. Ezequiel nods almost imperceptibly — confirming something he already suspected — and then puts two bullets through the television. "Get the trucks."

Benjamín pushes open the study door and freezes. Don Ezequiel is seated in Carmen's chair, turning the golden avocado over in his hands. Isabela is tied to a chair, surrounded by shattered glass. Ezequiel introduces himself: Ezequiel Antona. The initials from the photograph. The deal was simple: Carmen would hand over Grupo Serrano, and her grandchildren would walk away free. Ernesto's death and Benjamín's speech destroyed that arrangement.

Reynaldo pulls a plastic bag over Karla's head. Benjamín screams for them to wait. His eyes move — the golden avocado, Javier by the bar, the television still showing funeral coverage. Something shifts in his face. Not panic. Calculation. He tells Ezequiel he can help him. Then he asks the question out loud: is Javier going to be the face of Grupo Serrano? The man who can't talk to the Secretary of Agriculture without a PowerPoint deck. The man who doesn't know the names of the foremen. The man who has never set foot in an avocado grove. He lays out how Carmen made it work: she went to the events, the foundations, the board dinners, she put her face out there, and nobody — no government, no journalist, no American regulator — ever asked the right questions. "Who does that now? Does Javier go to the Super Bowl? Does Javier speak at the World Bank gala? Does Javier give an interview to Forbes and have anyone believe him?" Ezequiel looks at Javier. Javier has no answer. They both know it. Benjamín makes the offer: there are three of them. He handles the outside — investors, federal officials, international press. Karla knows every real number in the company. Isabela is the most human face they have. "You need three Serranos. Three faces with the right last name. Three people the world already knows, already trusts, already believes. Three Sopranos working for you while the world applauds. That's how the whole structure worked — and Carmen just died. We can fill that place. The place that lets you keep doing exactly what you want. What does the press say, what does law enforcement do, when the last heirs of Grupo Serrano turn up dead one morning? We're everything you need. And the one thing you can't manufacture." Ezequiel orders Reynaldo to let Karla go. He puts them under Javier's supervision and makes the terms clear: if they try to run, talk to anyone, or even dream of crossing him, he will break them.

When Ezequiel is gone, Karla asks Benjamín if he has a plan. Benjamín leans against the wall.

"The plan is us."

Isabela understands before Karla does.

"He's going to kill us anyway. Just slower."

The three siblings look at each other. Trapped. No visible way out. But this time the cage isn't empty — they built it themselves, with their own hands, in the moment they needed it most.
