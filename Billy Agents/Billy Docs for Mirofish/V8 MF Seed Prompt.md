Carmen Serrano — the matriarch of a billion-dollar Mexican avocado empire — has died, and in her final week, she removed her three grandchildren from her will. The night of her death, those grandchildren hired a thief to recover the disinheritance document from her lawyer’s safe. The lawyer came home. He died accidentally. And facing the cartel operator who co-built her empire, the middle sibling — Benjamín Serrano — gave a ninety-second speech that was entirely, devastatingly true: the Serrano name is the only thing keeping the criminal operation invisible, and without the family performing legitimacy, the entire infrastructure collapses. Don Ezequiel Antona accepted the offer. The siblings are now the public face of a narco operation they didn’t choose, didn’t know existed, and cannot escape without destroying everything.

Three audience groups are now in active collision. The legitimate world — investors, ESG journalists, multinational buyers, government contacts — sees Grupo Serrano’s new leadership and believes the family is clean. The investigation world — journalist Ingrid Cervantes, who knows Ernesto Vega’s death was not a burglary, and his grieving son Emilio, who has stayed longer than his return flight — is closing in from the outside without knowing the siblings are connected. And the criminal world — Don Ezequiel, his embedded CFO Javier Cordero, and an unnamed thief somewhere with a blue folder containing Carmen Serrano’s full forty-year archive — is watching every move the siblings make from the inside.

Simulate how these groups interact, conflict, and collide. Let the characters behave from who they are, not from what the story needs them to do. The most interesting questions are the ones this world has not yet answered: What is Javier Cordero actually building on his parallel track? Does the unnamed thief ever make contact — and if so, who finds them first? Does Carmen’s secret about the 2001 plane crash reach the siblings before Ingrid’s investigation does — or at the same moment? Which sibling breaks first, and in which direction?

At the end of the simulation, generate a report with four sections: “The Identity Engine” — how the performance evolved or fractured per sibling. “The Convergence” — how the external investigation progressed and what it triggered. “The Ghost of Carmen” — how her buried decisions moved through the world. “The Asymmetry” — how Ezequiel’s control held or cracked, and whether the loose end was ever activated. Include the simulation’s own emergent surprises — alliances, betrayals, and revelations that were not predictable from the starting positions.
