# ORO VERDE — Complete Show Bible & Pilot
## Reality Seed for MiroFish — V4

---

## Logline

Three privileged, emotionally broken siblings lose their inheritance when their dying grandmother removes them from the will — and their desperate attempt to steal it back accidentally kills Ernesto, the only man protecting them, leaving them owned by the same criminal empire that built the family fortune. The bomb under everything: their parents didn't die in an accident, and Carmen knew.

---

## Genre & Tone

Premium narco-family drama. Succession's dynastic warfare transplanted into Ozark's criminal captivity, with the moral rot of Narcos Mexico running underneath and the sensory texture of Drops of God — avocado instead of wine, but the same logic of inheritance and rot. The surface is immaculate: colonial haciendas, Macallan 18, Harvard MBAs, private jets. The violence is quiet and methodical. Nobody screams. The most brutal scenes happen without anyone raising their voice.

Emotionally contained but operatic in stakes. The Serrano siblings were raised not to cry in public. Rage looks like a quiet office door closing. Fear looks like checking your breath before walking into a boardroom. Grief looks like posting a smoothie photo on Instagram at 3am. The show's thesis: legitimate power and criminal power are the same thing in different clothes. The audience always knows more than the characters. That asymmetry is the engine of every scene.

---

## World & Setting

Contemporary Michoacán, Mexico, and Mexico City. The story lives inside the global avocado industry — Mexico produces over 30% of the world's supply, generating more than $3 billion USD annually in exports. This is narco-agricultural country, where the same mountains that grow "green gold" have been carved up by cartels for decades. The Serrano family's colonial hacienda is the world's physical center: beautiful enough to photograph, suffocating enough to hold you prisoner.

Mexico's dual economy is the show's structural spine. Grupo Serrano presents as a clean agribusiness — ESG commitments, board meetings, Javier, a CFO with an MBA from Harvard. Below that surface runs a criminal operation invisible to everyone, including the Serrano grandchildren. Carmen ran the legitimate empire. Don Ezequiel ran the narco infrastructure through the same avocado shipping and distribution channels — the same trucks, the same ports, the same cold-chain logistics, serving two masters simultaneously. Nobody inside Grupo Serrano ever knew. That was Carmen's most deliberate design. The political stakes are specific: labor exploitation in the ports of Lázaro Cárdenas, environmental devastation of Michoacán's forests and rivers, and the structural impossibility of building generational wealth in Mexico without a moral compromise that compounds across generations.

**CRITICAL CONTEXT FOR SIMULATION:** Until the final sequence of Episode 1, all three Serrano siblings believed with absolute certainty that their family fortune came entirely from avocados. Grupo Serrano was legitimate. Carmen was clean. The money was clean. They went to Harvard, drove German cars, and flew private on the proceeds of what they understood to be an exceptional agricultural business their grandmother built from nothing. The revelation that they are embedded in a criminal empire — and have been their entire lives without knowing it — lands on them with zero psychological preparation. They have no criminal instinct, no criminal vocabulary, no framework for navigating this world. They are not becoming criminals. This is the Ozark engine of the show: decent people (or people who thought they were decent) trapped in a system they didn't build and cannot escape without destroying everything they have left.

## CHARACTERS

### BENJAMÍN SERRANO — PROTAGONIST
**Age 35. Middle sibling. Enneagram Type 3w2 — The Performer.**

Benjamín has spent his entire life performing competence so convincingly he forgot it was a performance. The polished surface — the right jersey in Dallas, the pitch deck for the Texas billionaire, the calculated warmth in every room — conceals a man who doesn't know who he is when no one is watching. His survival is emotional: if he is succeeding, he exists; if he fails, he disappears. He was twelve years old when Ernesto told him his parents' private plane had gone down. He cried once, alone, and never again in front of anyone. Benjamin will soon learn that Lucia is the niece of Don Ezequiel Antona. And Don Ezequiel will use this against Benjamin.

**Want:** To prove he can run Grupo Serrano and be recognized as a legitimate leader — the heir Carmen never told him he was.
**Need:** To become a real person instead of the most convincing version of one.
**Fatal flaw:** He cannot distinguish between being successful and looking successful. He lies with the same ease he breathes.
**Secret:** To save Karla's life in the pilot he bluffed that he had in his possession an AI algorithm on the spot — a tool that supposedly maps supply routes, optimizes shipments, and evades inspections. Don Ezequiel believed it. The algorithm does not exist Benjamín now has to manage the consequences. It is not the season's engine — it is the wound he opened that bleeds in unpredictable ways.

**The criminal discovery:** Benjamín spent his career building a legitimate business identity — Harvard MBA, pitch decks, ESG language, dinner with Texas billionaires. The revelation that Grupo Serrano is a narco-distribution operation destroys the one thing he had that felt real: the belief that he was legitimately successful. He cannot perform his way out of this. There is no pitch deck for what he now faces. For the first time in his adult life, the gap between who he performs as and who he actually is may be impossible to bridge. This destabilizes him at the level of identity, not just strategy.

**Two simultaneous fault lines:** Benjamín enters Episode 2 carrying two irreconcilable crises that run in parallel for the rest of the season. The first is Don Ezequiel: he is now an asset in a criminal empire, managing a lie about a nonexistent algorithm, performing competence for a man who will kill him if the performance fails. The second is personal: Valeria, his wife, is arriving at the Hacienda with their one-year-old son, walking into a situation she has no framework to understand. And Lucía Suárez — the activist who opposed him in the boardroom and who is the first person in years who has made him feel something he doesn't have a word for — remains in his orbit. These women represent three incompatible futures, and Benjamín cannot choose between them because choosing would require being honest about who he is.

**Valeria context:** Benjamín did not fall in love with Valeria — he got her pregnant and married her because it was the correct performance of a man like him. He respects her. He is not cruel to her. But he has never chosen her, and she has always sensed this without being able to name it. Lucía is the first person who asks nothing of his performance and wants nothing from his name — which is exactly why he can't stop thinking about her.

**How he speaks:** Corporate fluency flipped casual. MBA vocabulary used to dominate rooms. Adapts his register to whoever he needs to seduce — cowboy slang with a Texas billionaire, boardroom authority with employees, calculated tenderness with the women who can see through him.
**Season arc:** From the man who fakes having everything under control to the man who must decide whether he deserves control of anything — including the truth about who killed his parents.

### KARLA SERRANO
**Age 40. Oldest sibling. Enneagram Type 8w7 — The Maverick.**

Karla took responsibility for her siblings the day her parents died and has been doing it so long she's forgotten she had a choice. She was seventeen. She never got to grieve because someone had to be strong. Her armor — aggressive control, physical dominance, strategic thinking — works in every room except the ones where she wants to be seen. She has built her entire identity around being unbreakable, and she chose a life that would never ask her to be anything else. Until Ingrid Cervantes, his ex girlfriend.

**Want:** To protect her family and maintain control of Grupo Serrano at any cost.
**Need:** To be loved without having to earn it by being the strongest person in the room.
**Fatal flaw:** She confuses armor with strength. She will destroy the relationship she needs in order to prove she doesn't need it.
**Secret:** She is re exploring the old relationship with Ingrid Cervantes, a journalist actively investigating the murder Karla is connected to. She has not told her siblings. She barely admits it to herself.

**The criminal discovery:** For Karla, the revelation that the family fortune is criminal lands differently than it does for Benjamín. She is a protector by nature — and what she discovers is that the thing she has spent twenty-three years protecting was already compromised before she started. She built her armor to keep the family safe. She is now learning that the danger was always inside the house. The plastic bag over her head — ordered by a man who sat at Carmen's table — is the physical emblem of how completely her model of the world has failed. An 8w7 who discovers her protection was an illusion doesn't collapse. She recalibrates toward aggression. The question is whether she can do it faster than Don Ezequiel can tighten the grip.

**How she speaks:** Declarative. Short sentences. Never qualifies. Uses silence as a weapon. When she's frightened, she speaks less, not more.
**Season arc:** From impenetrable to the first real crack — through Ingrid, the only person who tells her the truth without flinching, and the only person whose ethics and exposure could destroy all three of them.

### ISABELA SERRANO
**Age 24. Youngest sibling. Enneagram Type 7w6 — The Enthusiast.**

Isabela has always been the one no one takes seriously, which is exactly why she's the most dangerous. She was five when her parents died. She has no memory of them except what the family chose to give her — which means she has no grief to perform and no story to protect. She uses humor, beauty, and chaos as a shield against something she can't name. Her recovery from addiction gave her a capacity for stillness her siblings lack. She is the only one who actually looks at things.

**Want:** To find her real place in the world outside the family's expectations.
**Need:** To stop running from what she finds when she looks.
**Fatal flaw:** Escapism. She converts pain into adventure and loss into a lesson — until she can't.
**Secret:** In the pilot she found a 1982 photograph of Carmen in a field of red poppies — opium, not flowers — with the initials "CS y EA" and a ledger with eight-figure entries from the 1980s. She has told no one. She is the only sibling moving toward the truth that will rewrite everything they think they know.

**The criminal discovery:** Isabela's relationship to the revelation is uniquely strange. She was five when her parents died — she has no authentic memory of the family before the loss, only the version the family gave her. In one sense, she has less to lose: she never fully believed in the clean version the way Benjamín and Karla did. But she is also the one holding physical evidence of Carmen's criminal past — the photograph, the ledger — and she has said nothing. The discovery that the fortune was drug money doesn't destroy her worldview so much as it confirms a suspicion she has carried without words since childhood: that something in this family is not what they say it is. She is the only sibling who is not purely shocked. She is frightened, yes — but also vindicated. And now she has to decide whether to keep investigating alone or bring her siblings into what she already knows. She develops a forbidden romance with Emilio Vega, the late Ernesto's son who suspects that his father didn't die from an accident.

**How she speaks:** Rapid, associative, funny. Uses humor as deflection until something breaks through. Then goes very quiet.
**Season arc:** From the sibling everyone dismisses to the only one who understands what actually happened to this family — and must decide what to do with that knowledge.

### DOÑA CARMEN SERRANO — IN FLASHBACKS
**Age 81 at death. Present in flashbacks from age 14 through her final years. Enneagram Type 8w9 — La Osa.**

The woman who turned avocado into a billion-dollar empire. What nobody knows — not her grandchildren, not the lawyers, not the priest — is that Carmen co-built the criminal structure with Don Ezequiel from the beginning, using Grupo Serrano's distribution network to move product through avocado shipments. She ran the legal side. He ran the rest. In 2001, a rival cartel family targeted Carmen's daughter and son-in-law as leverage in a territorial war. Carmen knew the plane was going to crash. She did not stop it. She directed Don Ezequiel to destroy the rival family. She won the war. She consolidated power. She buried her daughter's real death under controlled grief and spent the remaining years of her life trying to free her grandchildren from the deal she made to protect them — disinheriting them as a last act of protection. She did not foresee that they would fight for the inheritance she removed. She died knowing she had failed.

**Want:** To protect her grandchildren from the consequences of everything she built.
**Need:** To admit, to someone, what she actually did.
**Fatal flaw:** She cannot distinguish between protecting people and controlling them. She loved like a general — by moving pieces.
**How she speaks:** Sparse. Every sentence lands. She doesn't explain. She announces. In flashbacks she moves from fierce and principled to something harder to name: a woman who knows exactly what she has become and has decided the alternative was worse.
**Season arc (in flashbacks):** From the fourteen-year-old girl defending her mother from a drunk father to the woman who let her own daughter's plane go down and called it strategy.

---

### DON EZEQUIEL ANTONA — ANTAGONIST
**Age 65. Enneagram Type 8w9 — El Oso.**

Don Ezequiel is Carmen's mirror — same type, same wing, different roads. He built a criminal empire embedded inside Grupo Serrano's distribution infrastructure for thirty years. He doesn't intimidate with volume. He intimidates with patience. The plastic bag over Karla's head he ordered without standing up. He made the original deal with Carmen as an equal — two Type 8s who recognized each other and decided partnership was more efficient than war. Now Carmen is dead and three amateurs are sitting on the throne he co-built. He doesn't see this as a crisis. He sees it as an opportunity. The grandchildren don't know what Carmen knew. That gap is his leverage.

**Want:** Full operational control of Grupo Serrano and uninterrupted use of its distribution network.
**Need:** Nothing he's willing to admit to anyone, including himself.
**Fatal flaw:** The arrogance of the man who has never been surprised. He cannot imagine being outmaneuvered by people simpler than him.
**Secret:** He knows the siblings didn't cause Ernesto's death deliberately. He's letting them believe they did. The guilt is the leash.
**Key advantage over the siblings:** Don Ezequiel understands that the three Serranos have just discovered the criminal nature of their family empire. He uses their disorientation deliberately — feeding them just enough information to keep them compliant, withholding enough to keep them afraid. He has managed criminals for decades. He has never managed amateurs who believe they are innocent. That novelty is the source of his only real vulnerability.
**How he speaks:** Unhurried. Uses the pause as punctuation. Makes statements that require answers. Refers to violence in the passive voice.

### JAVIER CORDERO — THE INSIDER
**Age 40. CFO of Grupo Serrano. Enneagram Type 5w6 — The Strategist.**

Harvard MBA, bespoke suits, smile like a spreadsheet. Don Ezequiel's embedded analyst inside Grupo Serrano for years — accumulating information with the precision of a man who knows that knowledge withheld at the right moment is a weapon. He doesn't need to threaten Benjamín. He dismantles him with budget line items in front of witnesses. He reports every sibling move to Don Ezequiel before they've finished making it.

**Want:** Real power — to be the actual operator behind whoever sits in the CEO chair.
**Need:** To admit he is not above the system he serves. He is its instrument.
**Fatal flaw:** He believes he controls Don Ezequiel because he understands him analytically. He doesn't.
**How he speaks:** Clinical. Cites data before making any point. The MBA is his native language and his primary weapon.

---

### LUCÍA SUÁREZ — BENJAMÍN'S CONSCIENCE
**Age 35. Environmental activist. Enneagram Type 1w2 — The Defender.**

Lucía fights against Grupo Serrano's destruction of Michoacán's rivers and forests with a megaphone, a live feed, and a moral clarity that makes Benjamín feel transparent. She is the only character in the show who says exactly what she thinks without political calculation — which makes her the most disorienting person in his world. He is trained to read people. She is unreadable because she has no hidden agenda. Their attraction is real on both sides and inconvenient to both of them in entirely different ways. 

**Want:** A sustainable avocado industry that doesn't destroy the communities it depends on.
**Need:** To allow for human complexity without abandoning her principles.
**Fatal flaw:** She sees in black and white. The grey zone where Benjamín lives is, to her, simply moral failure — until it stops being simple.
**How she speaks:** Direct, specific, grounded in concrete examples. Knows the name of every family whose land was taken. Never uses abstractions.
**Season function:** Thermometer for Benjamín's corruption. Every step he takes toward Don Ezequiel is a step away from her. The audience feels the cost. She is also the only person in Benjamín's orbit who has no idea what he has entered — which makes her simultaneously the safest and most dangerous person for him to be near.

---

### EMILIO VEGA — THE INVESTIGATOR
**Age 36. Son of Ernesto Vega. Enneagram Type 9w8 — The Arbitrator.**

Emilio arrives in Michoacán at the start of Episode 2 to close his father Ernesto's estate and return home. He is not a detective. He is not looking for a fight. He came with a folder of legal documents and a return flight booked for the following week. But the details don't add up: a family lawyer with forty years of spotless professional history is murdered in his own home during what appears to be a burglary — and the only thing taken was a single blue folder from a specialist safe hidden behind a painting. Emilio is a 9 who moves toward peace by nature. But his dormant 8 wing wakes up the longer he stays in Michoacán, and once it wakes up, it does not go back to sleep.

**Want:** To understand why his father died. Then go home.
**Need:** To accept that the truth will change him, and that this is not a reason to stop.
**Fatal flaw:** He minimizes what he doesn't want to see. His instinct for peace can become a mechanism for looking away — until the moment it can't.
**Episode 2 entry point:** Emilio begins his investigation almost accidentally. He visits the house. He talks to neighbors. He asks questions that seem like grief and are becoming something else. His path will cross Ingrid Cervantes' before either of them realizes they are looking at the same thing from different angles. His involuntary alliance with her creates the most sustained external threat to the Serrano siblings: two people searching for the truth out of love, not money — which means they cannot be bought and they do not have a price.
**How he speaks:** Careful, measured. Gets quieter as he gets angrier.
**Season arc:** From a man who came to close a chapter to a man who cannot close his eyes — converging with Isabela's investigation from the outside in.

---

### INGRID CERVANTES — KARLA'S MIRROR/ROMANTIC INTEREST
**Age 45. Investigative journalist and TV anchor. Enneagram Type 1w9 — The Idealist.**

Ingrid appears in the pilot as the voice narrating Carmen Serrano's death. Her investigation into Ernesto Vega's murder isn't just what brings her to the hacienda. What brings her back is Karla. Her journalism is philosophical as well as moral — she doesn't just want to report what happened, she wants to understand why the world is structured to produce it. She says true things to Karla that nobody else would dare say. Karla doesn't know what to do with someone who doesn't move.

**Want:** The truth about Ernesto Vega's death and what it connects to.
**Need:** To accept that the truth and the person she fell in love with are the same story.
**Fatal flaw:** Her moral rigidity — the quality that makes her a great journalist — can become a blindness when a human being she cares about stops fitting the story she's telling.
**Secret:** She already knows more about the Serrano family than she has revealed. She is deciding how to use it.
**How she speaks:** Measured. Asks the question beneath the question. In conversation with Karla, she is the only person who holds eye contact longer than Karla does.
**Season function:** External pressure on the siblings' secrecy through Karla's emotional vulnerability. The investigation and the relationship are the same bomb.

### VALERIA LUNA — BENJAMÍN'S WIFE
**Age 30. Architect. From a clean Monterrey family.**

Valeria did not marry a man. She married a version of a man — the version Benjamín performs best: competent, warm, going somewhere. She got pregnant during what she thought was a serious relationship. Benjamín married her because it was the correct thing for a man like him to do. He is not cruel to her. He respects her. He shows up to the performances that matter. But he has never chosen her — not in the way she chose him — and she has always sensed this without being able to name it.

In Episode 2, Valeria arrives at the Hacienda with their one-year-old son. She does not know what the family has entered. She walks in reading the room — the tension in Gabriela's posture, the way Benjamín holds her slightly too long at the door, the unfamiliar men she hasn't been introduced to — and she cannot yet assemble the information into a complete picture. She is smart. She will.

**Want:** Her husband back — the real one, or the one she believed was real.
**Need:** To trust what she senses rather than what she's told.
**Fatal flaw:** She conflates patience with love. She waits when she should leave.
**What she doesn't know:** She doesn't know the family fortune is criminal. She doesn't know about Lucía. She doesn't know what Don Ezequiel is or what he wants. She is operating with less information than anyone in the Hacienda — and she is the most perceptive person in the room.
**How she speaks:** Precise, architectural. Describes feelings the way she would describe a structural problem — accurately, without sentimentality.
**Season function:** Moral cost meter for Benjamín. The more he disappears into Don Ezequiel's world, the more present and real she becomes to the audience — and the more impossible she is for him to face directly. Her presence at the Hacienda makes Lucía impossible to pursue and Don Ezequiel impossible to hide.

### GABRIELA RUIZ — THE GATEKEEPER
**Age 63. Head housekeeper of the Hacienda. 40 years in Carmen's service.**

Gabriela has cleaned around every secret in this family for four decades. She knows the full institutional history — the ledgers, the photographs, the arrangement with Don Ezequiel, and what really happened to the parents' plane. Carmen never told her directly. Decades of proximity made concealment impossible.

**Want:** To protect the grandchildren the way Carmen asked her to — without ever having to say why.
**Need:** To put down a burden she never agreed to carry.
**Fatal flaw:** Loyalty that has become self-erasure. She has confused serving Carmen with being Carmen.
**How she speaks:** Only when spoken to. Her silences hold more information than her words. When she has to lie, she goes very still.

---

### PADRE MENDOZA
**Age 72. Parish priest of the Hacienda village.**

The oldest living person who knows the full shape of what Carmen built. Was present in the 1980s. Has heard confession from at least one person directly involved in the events of 2001. Has managed that knowledge through prayer and silence for decades.

**Want:** For God to have already judged this — so he doesn't have to.
**Need:** To accept that silence in the face of injustice is its own kind of complicity.
**How he speaks:** Biblical cadence. Never directly. One straight sentence carries the weight of a full confession.

## PILOT SYNOPSIS — Episode 101: "Echados a Perder"

**COLD OPEN:** Isabela's face, blood-smeared, reflected in a fractured mirror. A man's hand lifts her by the hair. Everything seen through the reflection — never direct. SUPER: 6 DAYS EARLIER.

**ACT ONE:** Benjamín pitches a speculative investment deal to a Texas billionaire in a Cowboys luxury box, wearing the team jersey as a costume. The billionaire wants to know what Carmen thinks. Benjamín lies. Meanwhile: Karla watches Ingrid Cervantes on television from her jacuzzi until her lover Sophia enters and she cuts the feed. Isabela boards a private jet alone, clutching a sobriety chip.

**ACT TWO:** Ernesto is intercepted at the hacienda gate by Don Ezequiel's men — calm, menacing, deliberate. Inside, Carmen receives her grandchildren with brutal clarity: she's cutting them out of the inheritance. "¡Es mi dinero!" A bloody cough ends the meeting.

**ACT THREE:** The siblings plan to steal the blue folder from Ernesto's safe. Isabela discovers the 1982 photograph and the ledger while alone with Carmen — hides them when the cardiac monitor alarms. In the boardroom, Benjamín offers community compensation for environmental damage. Javier walks in and dismantles every number. Lucía leaves him with: "¿Sabes qué es peor que no hacer nada? Hacer como que te importa."

**ACT FOUR:** At night, a hired figure cracks Ernesto's safe behind a Remedios Varo painting, takes the folder, hears footsteps upstairs. Freezes.

**ACT FIVE:** Morning. Ernesto is dead. Carmen dies the same day. A single flashback: twelve-year-old Benjamín running toward the hacienda smiling, confused by the black clothes on everyone. Ernesto was the one who told him his parents' plane had gone down. Carmen watches from a window above. Her face: a door closing from the inside.

**ACT SIX:** At the memorial, Javier tries to block Benjamín from the podium. Karla physically stops him. Benjamín claims the company publicly. At his ranch, Don Ezequiel watches on television. His cigar snaps between his fingers: "Tráete las camionetas."

**FINAL SEQUENCE:** Benjamín finds Don Ezequiel sitting in Carmen's chair, turning the gold avocado in his hands. Don Ezequiel explains the deal — Carmen gave him operational use of Grupo Serrano to keep the grandchildren free. The theft and the speech tore up that agreement. Reynaldo puts a plastic bag over Karla's head. Benjamín, with nothing left to offer, invents one: an AI algorithm that maps supply routes, optimizes shipments, evades inspection patterns. Don Ezequiel stops Reynaldo. Issues a warning they believe completely. When he leaves, Karla asks if the algorithm is real. Benjamín slides down the wall. "No hay nada." CORTE A NEGRO.

**END STATE — WHAT EVERY CHARACTER KNOWS AS SIMULATION BEGINS:**
- All three siblings now know: their family fortune was built on drug money run through Grupo Serrano's avocado distribution network. They believed for their entire lives that it came from a legitimate agricultural business their grandmother built. This was a lie.
- Benjamín knows: the AI algorithm he promised Don Ezequiel does not exist. He has days or weeks before this becomes a crisis.
- Karla knows: Don Ezequiel will hurt them without hesitation. She carries the physical memory of the plastic bag.
- Isabela knows: the 1982 photograph and ledger she found connect Carmen directly to Don Ezequiel's initials. She has told no one.
- Javier knows: everything. And reports it all.
- Don Ezequiel knows: the algorithm may be a lie, but he cannot be certain. He is watching.
- Emilio Vega does not yet know his father was murdered rather than killed in a robbery. He will arrive in Episode 2 to find out.
- Valeria does not know any of this. She will arrive at the Hacienda in Episode 2 with their one-year-old son.

## SEASON THEMATIC SPINE

What is the price of an empire built on blood — and who pays it when the person who built it is gone? The season asks this from three angles simultaneously. Benjamín is the man who fakes his way into power and discovers that power demands something real, not performed. Karla is the woman who built armor instead of a life and is meeting, for the first time, someone who won't pretend the armor is enough. Isabela is the only one willing to look at what the family actually is — and the only one moving toward the truth that will break all three of them.

Carmen's flashback arc is the tragic inversion: a woman who started with principles and made one compromise, then another, then one that cannot be undone. The bomb at the center of the season — detonating at the midpoint — is the revelation that the siblings' parents did not die in an accident. Their private plane went down because Carmen allowed it to happen: she knew a rival cartel had targeted her daughter and son-in-law, chose the empire over her child, won the war, and never told anyone. The grief the siblings have carried for twenty-three years is built on a lie. Everything they thought they knew about their family detonates in Episode 5.

**Moral question:** When you didn't choose the crime you inherited, how responsible are you for it? And when the people who built that crime are gone — do you have the right to the truth about what they were?

---

## SEASON-LONG CONFLICT

Don Ezequiel expected a clean transition. Instead he inherited three amateurs who don't know the rules of the world they've entered — and who are therefore unpredictable in ways Carmen never was. The season runs on three converging pressure lines. First, Isabela's investigation of the 1982 photograph moves toward the midpoint revelation: the plane was not an accident, Carmen knew, and the initials on the photograph connect directly to Don Ezequiel. Second, Emilio Vega and Ingrid Cervantes are moving toward the same truth from outside — through Ernesto's murder, through the Grupo Serrano paper trail, through each other. Third, Don Ezequiel is slowly recognizing that the grandchildren cannot be managed the way Carmen was, because they have no idea what they're actually protecting — which means they might destroy it without meaning to.

When the midpoint revelation breaks, the siblings are no longer passive. They know what was done to their parents. They know Carmen's role. They know Don Ezequiel's. The final four episodes are about what three people with that knowledge, trapped inside that system, choose to do with it.

## THE WORLD'S RULES

**Power** is information withheld at the right moment. Carmen had it. Javier has it. Isabela is learning it.
**Loyalty** is the most expensive currency in this world — and everyone inflates their claims on it. Ernesto's cost him his life.
**Betrayal** is punished with exposure before violence. Don Ezequiel doesn't kill sloppily. He makes examples methodically.
**The family** is the only unit of safety — and the primary source of danger. Both things are always true at the same time.
**The law** exists as costume. Investigations go as far as Don Ezequiel permits.
**The narco infrastructure** is invisible by design — it lives inside the trucks, the ports, the cold-chain logistics of a legitimate avocado empire.
**Appearance** is moral currency. The Serranos must look clean while becoming criminal.
**The past** is not finished. Every decision Carmen made is still running. The siblings are living inside consequences they didn't choose, made by a woman they thought they knew.
**Ignorance is not innocence.** The siblings didn't know. But they are now inside the system. Every day they stay, they become more complicit — whether they choose to or not.

## COMPARABLE SHOWS

- **Ozark (Netflix):** Structural DNA — a family of non-criminals forced into cartel servitude through one catastrophic mistake, each episode compounding their complicity. Oro Verde shares the claustrophobic captivity and the question of how far people who consider themselves decent will go to survive. The Ozark engine is precisely the Oro Verde engine: ordinary people discovering that the world they thought they lived in was always criminal.
- **Succession (HBO):** Dynastic warfare within a family, corporate protocol used as emotional violence, and the dark comedy of hyper-privileged people completely out of their depth. Oro Verde inherits Succession's central thesis: family legacy is a weapon everyone uses against everyone else.
- **Narcos Mexico:** The cartel world portrayed with moral complexity and internal logic. Don Ezequiel is not a monster — he's an institution. Oro Verde uses the same methodology.
- **Drops of God (Apple TV+):** The luxury commodity at the center — avocado as green gold — functions the way wine does in that series: as a vehicle for inheritance, obsession, and the question of what we owe the people who built the worlds we live in.

## TARGET AUDIENCE

**Primary:** Mexican and US Latino streaming audiences on Netflix MX / ViX+. Ages 28-48, professional class, bilingual comfort. The show mirrors how upper-class Mexican families actually speak — Spanish with corporate English code-switching. Both genders, skewing slightly female due to the emotional complexity of the sibling arcs.

**Secondary:** International prestige drama audiences who followed Narcos, Money Heist, and Who Killed Sara. The English-language comp DNA makes it crossover-ready without requiring the cultural specificity to be softened. Episode 5 — the midpoint revelation — is the episode that gets clipped, shared, and talked about.
