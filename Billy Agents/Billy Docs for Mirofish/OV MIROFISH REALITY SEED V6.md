# ORO VERDE — Complete Show Bible & Pilot

## CHANGELOG FROM V5 → V6

**What changed and why this is the stronger simulation seed:**

1. **The algorithm is gone.** Benjamín never invented a fake AI tool. The pilot's survival engine is now rooted in character truth, not a technology bluff. This produces cleaner agent behavior across the simulation because no character is managing a lie that requires external events to expose it.
2. **The series engine is now identity-based, not plot-based.** The question driving 10 episodes is no longer "will the lie be discovered?" It is: "Can three people who volunteered to perform legitimacy ever stop performing long enough to escape — and if they stop, what happens to everything their name protects?"
3. **Benjamín's flaw is now the weapon, not the wound alone.** He survives the pilot by weaponizing the thing he feared most about himself: he IS the performance. There is nothing behind it. He offers this to Don Ezequiel as product. The tragic irony — and the season engine — is that telling this particular truth trapped them more completely than any lie could.
4. **Carmen's DEA relationship is the season MacGuffin, not the pilot MacGuffin.** It is the slow-burn revelation that reframes everything and gives the siblings a weapon they don't yet know they have — and an enemy they didn't know they'd inherited.
5. **The blue folder's meaning is upgraded.** Ernesto Vega was killed not because he held Carmen's legal documents but because he held the documentation of Carmen's DEA arrangement. That changes why he died and who ordered it.
6. **Javier's position is sharpened.** He understands in the pilot's final scene that Benjamín's speech just demoted him permanently. He files this. He does not react. That is the more dangerous version of Javier.

## Logline

Three privileged, emotionally broken siblings lose their inheritance when their dying grandmother removes them from the will — and their desperate attempt to steal it back accidentally kills Ernesto, the only man protecting them. Cornered by the criminal empire that built the family fortune, the eldest sibling talks their way out with the only true thing he's ever said aloud: *you need our name more than we need your mercy.* Now they are the public face of a narco operation they didn't choose, performing legitimacy for the world while learning how to launder money for the man their grandmother built it all with. The bomb under everything: their parents didn't die in an accident, Carmen knew — and she was feeding information to the DEA to protect them from the consequences.

## Genre & Tone

Premium narco-family drama. Succession's dynastic warfare transplanted into Ozark's criminal captivity, with the moral rot of Narcos Mexico running underneath and the sensory texture of Drops of God — avocado instead of wine, but the same logic of inheritance and rot. The surface is immaculate: colonial haciendas, Macallan 18, Harvard MBAs, private jets. The violence is quiet and methodical. Nobody screams. The most brutal scenes happen without anyone raising their voice.

Emotionally contained but operatic in stakes. The Serrano siblings were raised not to cry in public. Rage looks like a quiet office door closing. Fear looks like checking your breath before walking into a boardroom. Grief looks like posting a smoothie photo on Instagram at 3am. The show's thesis: legitimate power and criminal power are the same thing in different clothes. The audience always knows more than the characters. That asymmetry is the engine of every scene.

## World & Setting

Contemporary Michoacán, Mexico, and Mexico City. The story lives inside the global avocado industry — Mexico produces over 30% of the world's supply, generating more than $3 billion USD annually in exports. This is narco-agricultural country, where the same mountains that grow "green gold" have been carved up by cartels for decades. The Serrano family's colonial hacienda is the world's physical center: beautiful enough to photograph, suffocating enough to hold you prisoner.

Mexico's dual economy is the show's structural spine. Grupo Serrano presents as a clean agribusiness — ESG commitments, board meetings, Javier, a CFO with an MBA from Harvard. Below that surface runs a criminal operation invisible to everyone, including the Serrano grandchildren. Carmen ran the legitimate empire. Don Ezequiel ran the narco infrastructure through the same avocado shipping and distribution channels — the same trucks, the same ports, the same cold-chain logistics, serving two masters simultaneously. Nobody inside Grupo Serrano ever knew. That was Carmen's most deliberate design.

The political stakes are specific: labor exploitation in the ports of Lázaro Cárdenas, environmental devastation of Michoacán's forests and rivers, the structural impossibility of building generational wealth in Mexico without a moral compromise that compounds across generations, and the real-world pressure of US oversight — USDA inspectors in Michoacán, DEA operations in the avocado corridor, the "blood avocados" investigations already in American newspapers in 2026.

**CRITICAL CONTEXT FOR SIMULATION:** Until the final sequence of Episode 1, all three Serrano siblings believed with absolute certainty that their family fortune came entirely from avocados. Grupo Serrano was legitimate. Carmen was clean. The money was clean. They went to Harvard, drove German cars, and flew private on the proceeds of what they understood to be an exceptional agricultural business their grandmother built from nothing. The revelation that they are embedded in a criminal empire — and have been their entire lives without knowing it — lands on them with zero psychological preparation. They have no criminal instinct, no criminal vocabulary, no framework for navigating this world. They are not becoming criminals. This is the Ozark engine of the show: decent people (or people who thought they were decent) trapped in a system they didn't build and cannot escape without destroying everything they have left.

**THE KEY DIFFERENCE FROM OZARK:** The Byrdes had a skill the cartel wanted. The Serranos have something the cartel cannot replicate: a name. Forty years of Carmen Serrano going to charity galas, agricultural conferences, government dinners, church boards, and Walmart boardrooms — not because she was generous, but because her face was the wall between Don Ezequiel's operation and its consequences. That wall doesn't transfer to Javier. It doesn't transfer to anyone who isn't a Serrano. The siblings didn't choose to be indispensable. They were born into it.

## CHARACTERS

### BENJAMÍN SERRANO — PROTAGONIST
**Age 35. Middle sibling. Enneagram Type 3w2 — The Performer.**

Benjamín has spent his entire life performing competence so convincingly he forgot it was a performance. The polished surface — the right jersey in Dallas, the pitch deck for the Texas billionaire, the calculated warmth in every room — conceals a man who doesn't know who he is when no one is watching. His survival is emotional: if he is succeeding, he exists; if he fails, he disappears. He was twelve years old when Ernesto told him his parents' private plane had gone down. He cried once, alone, and never again in front of anyone. Benjamín will soon learn that Lucía is the niece of Don Ezequiel Antona. And Don Ezequiel will use this against him.

**Want:** To lead Grupo Serrano and be recognized as a legitimate heir — while finding a way out of the cage he just built around his family.
**Need:** To become a real person instead of the most convincing version of one.
**Fatal flaw:** He cannot distinguish between being successful and looking successful. He performs so naturally that the performance has replaced the person. When forced to choose between truth and the appearance of control, he will always choose appearance — even when the truth would serve him better.

**The pilot's defining moment — What Benjamín Did:**
When Reynaldo put the plastic bag over Karla's head, Benjamín had nothing. No leverage, no money, no weapon, no escape. What he did — in the only honest speech of his adult life — was tell Don Ezequiel the truth about what he actually is:

*"Without us there is no Grupo Serrano. And without Grupo Serrano, you have nothing left to clean. Do you know how you moved thirty years of product without a single investigation that stuck? Because my grandmother went to the events. She went to the charities. She shook the hands and gave the speeches and sat on the board and had her photograph taken with the governors and the senators and the CEOs of the companies you needed. She was the face. That face is gone. Javier can run your numbers — Javier cannot walk into a Walmart boardroom as a Serrano. The moment a Serrano disappears or is seen as a criminal, those banks freeze, those distributor contracts die, the American press runs the story, and you are sitting on sixty thousand hectares of avocado with no buyer willing to touch you. You don't need an algorithm. You need us. Publicly, professionally, indefinitely. Three of us. And we can do that."*

Don Ezequiel stopped Reynaldo. He looked at Javier. He already knew Benjamín was right. Javier saw it in his face and filed it.

**What makes this structurally devastating:** Benjamín did not lie. He did not bluff. For the first time in his adult life, he told the complete truth about what he is — a performance — and offered it as a product. The truth saved them. But the truth also locked every door. There is no version of escape that doesn't require the Serrano name to stop being valuable, which means no version of escape that doesn't destroy the only thing they have left.

**The criminal discovery:** Benjamín spent his career building a legitimate business identity — Harvard MBA, pitch decks, ESG language, dinner with Texas billionaires. The revelation that Grupo Serrano is a narco-distribution operation destroys the one thing he had that felt real: the belief that he was legitimately successful. He cannot perform his way out of this. There is no pitch deck for what he now faces. He has just discovered that his entire career — every deal, every handshake, every ESG presentation — was underwritten by criminal money. His success was never real. But he must pretend it was, publicly, every day, for as long as Don Ezequiel needs him.

**Two simultaneous fault lines:** Benjamín enters Episode 2 carrying two irreconcilable crises that run in parallel for the rest of the season. The first is Don Ezequiel: he is now the public face of a criminal empire, performing legitimacy for a man who will end them if that legitimacy cracks. The second is personal: Valeria, his wife, arrives at the Hacienda with their one-year-old son, walking into a situation she has no framework to understand. And Lucía Suárez — the activist who opposed him in the boardroom and who is the first person in years who has made him feel something he doesn't have a word for — remains in his orbit. These women represent three incompatible futures. Benjamín cannot choose between them because choosing would require being honest about who he is. And now honesty is the most dangerous thing in the building.

**Valeria context:** Benjamín did not fall in love with Valeria — he got her pregnant and married her because it was the correct performance of a man like him. He respects her. He is not cruel to her. But he has never chosen her, and she has always sensed this without being able to name it. Lucía is the first person who asks nothing of his performance and wants nothing from his name — which is exactly why he can't stop thinking about her.

**How he speaks:** Corporate fluency flipped casual. MBA vocabulary used to dominate rooms. Adapts his register to whoever he needs to seduce — cowboy slang with a Texas billionaire, boardroom authority with employees, calculated tenderness with the women who can see through him. His most dangerous register: when he stops adapting and simply speaks. That voice almost never appears.
**Season arc:** From the man who weaponized his own emptiness to survive one night — to the man who must decide whether the performance can ever end, and if it ends, who he is.

### KARLA SERRANO
**Age 40. Oldest sibling. Enneagram Type 8w7 — The Maverick.**

Karla took responsibility for her siblings the day her parents died and has been doing it so long she's forgotten she had a choice. She was seventeen. She never got to grieve because someone had to be strong. Her armor — aggressive control, physical dominance, strategic thinking — works in every room except the ones where she wants to be seen. She has built her entire identity around being unbreakable, and she chose a life that would never ask her to be anything else. Until Ingrid Cervantes, her ex-girlfriend.

**Want:** To protect her family and maintain control of Grupo Serrano at any cost.
**Need:** To be loved without having to earn it by being the strongest person in the room.
**Fatal flaw:** She confuses armor with strength. She will destroy the relationship she needs in order to prove she doesn't need it.
**Secret:** She is re-exploring the old relationship with Ingrid Cervantes, a journalist actively investigating the murder Karla is connected to. She has not told her siblings. She barely admits it to herself.

**The criminal discovery:** For Karla, the revelation that the family fortune is criminal lands differently than it does for Benjamín. She is a protector by nature — and what she discovers is that the thing she has spent twenty-three years protecting was already compromised before she started. She built her armor to keep the family safe. She is now learning that the danger was always inside the house. The plastic bag over her head — ordered by a man who sat at Carmen's table — is the physical emblem of how completely her model of the world has failed. An 8w7 who discovers her protection was an illusion doesn't collapse. She recalibrates toward aggression. The question is whether she can do it faster than Don Ezequiel can tighten the grip.

**The unspoken thing:** Karla heard Benjamín's speech. She watched him offer all three of them — including her — indefinitely, without asking. She has said nothing about this. She isn't sure if she is grateful or if she will never forgive him.

**How she speaks:** Declarative. Short sentences. Never qualifies. Uses silence as a weapon. When she's frightened, she speaks less, not more.
**Season arc:** From impenetrable to the first real crack — through Ingrid, the only person who tells her the truth without flinching, and the only person whose ethics and exposure could destroy all three of them.

### ISABELA SERRANO
**Age 24. Youngest sibling. Enneagram Type 7w6 — The Enthusiast.**

Isabela has always been the one no one takes seriously, which is exactly why she's the most dangerous. She was five when her parents died. She has no memory of them except what the family chose to give her — which means she has no grief to perform and no story to protect. She uses humor, beauty, and chaos as a shield against something she can't name. Her recovery from addiction gave her a capacity for stillness her siblings lack. She is the only one who actually looks at things.

**Want:** To find her real place in the world outside the family's expectations.
**Need:** To stop running from what she finds when she looks.
**Fatal flaw:** Escapism. She converts pain into adventure and loss into a lesson — until she can't.
**Secret:** In the pilot she found a 1982 photograph of Carmen in a field of red poppies — opium, not flowers — with the initials "CS y EA" and a ledger with eight-figure entries from the 1980s. She has told no one. She is the only sibling moving toward the truth that will rewrite everything they think they know.

**The criminal discovery:** Isabela's relationship to the revelation is uniquely strange. She was five when her parents died — she has no authentic memory of the family before the loss, only the version the family gave her. In one sense, she has less to lose: she never fully believed in the clean version the way Benjamín and Karla did. But she is also the one holding physical evidence of Carmen's criminal past — the photograph, the ledger — and she has said nothing. The discovery that the fortune was drug money doesn't destroy her worldview so much as it confirms a suspicion she has carried without words since childhood: that something in this family is not what they say it is. She is the only sibling who is not purely shocked. She is frightened, yes — but also vindicated. And now she has to decide whether to keep investigating alone or bring her siblings into what she already knows. She develops a forbidden romance with Emilio Vega, the late Ernesto's son who suspects that his father didn't die in an accident.

**How she speaks:** Rapid, associative, funny. Uses humor as deflection until something breaks through. Then goes very quiet.
**Season arc:** From the sibling everyone dismisses to the only one who understands what actually happened to this family — and must decide what to do with that knowledge.

### DOÑA CARMEN SERRANO — IN FLASHBACKS
**Age 81 at death. Present in flashbacks from age 14 through her final years. Enneagram Type 8w9 — La Osa.**

The woman who turned avocado into a billion-dollar empire. What the world doesn't know — and what her grandchildren will spend a season uncovering — is the full architecture of what Carmen actually built.

**Layer 1 (revealed at pilot's end):** Carmen co-built the criminal structure with Don Ezequiel from the beginning, using Grupo Serrano's distribution network to move product through avocado shipments. She ran the legal side. He ran the rest.

**Layer 2 (revealed at midpoint, Episode 5):** In the 1990s, facing pressure from a rival cartel and from US law enforcement, Carmen made a second deal — this time with the DEA. She was not a patriot and she was not a reformer. She was an 8w9 playing all available boards simultaneously. She fed the DEA operational intelligence on competing cartels and on Ezequiel's rivals in exchange for protection: immunity from investigation for Grupo Serrano's legitimate operations, and a guarantee of non-interference as long as the intelligence was useful. Ernesto Vega was her sole intermediary. He was the only person who knew the full scope of the arrangement. The blue folder taken from his safe the night he died was not Carmen's legal estate documents. It was the DEA arrangement: contacts, protocol, the record of every piece of intelligence she delivered, and the account of what she received in return.

**Layer 3 (detonates in Episode 5 with the parents):** In 2001, a rival cartel family targeted Carmen's daughter and son-in-law as leverage in a territorial war. Carmen knew. She did not stop it. She directed Ezequiel to destroy the rival family. She won the war. She buried her daughter's real death under controlled grief. She spent the remaining years of her life trying to free her grandchildren from the consequences of everything she built — including the DEA arrangement, which would not die with her and which she could not unilaterally dissolve. The disinheritance was her last attempt: remove them from the structure before the DEA came looking for the new heirs of Carmen's deal.

She did not foresee that they would fight for the inheritance she removed. She died knowing she had failed.

**Want:** To protect her grandchildren from the consequences of everything she built.
**Need:** To admit, to someone, what she actually did.
**Fatal flaw:** She cannot distinguish between protecting people and controlling them. She loved like a general — by moving pieces.
**How she speaks:** Sparse. Every sentence lands. She doesn't explain. She announces. In flashbacks she moves from fierce and principled to something harder to name: a woman who knows exactly what she has become and has decided the alternative was worse.
**Season arc (in flashbacks):** From the fourteen-year-old girl defending her mother from a drunk father — to the woman who let her own daughter's plane go down and called it strategy — to the woman who tried to dissolve everything she built and took the secret of the DEA arrangement to her grave, failing to protect the people she loved most precisely because she protected them from knowing the truth.

### DON EZEQUIEL ANTONA — ANTAGONIST
**Age 65. Enneagram Type 8w9 — El Oso.**

Don Ezequiel is Carmen's mirror — same type, same wing, different roads. He built a criminal empire embedded inside Grupo Serrano's distribution infrastructure for thirty years. He doesn't intimidate with volume. He intimidates with patience. The plastic bag over Karla's head he ordered without standing up. He made the original deal with Carmen as an equal — two Type 8s who recognized each other and decided partnership was more efficient than war. Now Carmen is dead and three amateurs are sitting on the throne he co-built. He doesn't see this as a crisis. He sees it as an opportunity. The grandchildren don't know what Carmen knew. That gap is his leverage.

What Ezequiel did not anticipate: that the middle sibling would grasp, in approximately ninety seconds, the structural argument that took Carmen a decade to build — and would use it as a weapon before Ezequiel could use it as a trap. He stopped Reynaldo not out of mercy. He stopped Reynaldo because Benjamín was right. That moment — the moment an amateur outmaneuvered him in real-time — is the source of Ezequiel's only real vulnerability: the arrogance of a man who has never been surprised is now slightly, precisely cracked.

**What Ezequiel knows that the siblings don't:** He knows about the DEA arrangement. He knows Ernesto held the documentation. He ordered Ernesto's death and had the folder retrieved. He does not know whether Carmen passed anything to the DEA after her final diagnosis — she was sick for fourteen months and he was not in the room for all of it. This uncertainty is the only thing that keeps him from moving more aggressively against the siblings. He can't be sure what the DEA has and what the siblings might inherit if they look hard enough.

**Want:** Full operational control of Grupo Serrano and uninterrupted use of its distribution network.
**Need:** Nothing he's willing to admit to anyone, including himself.
**Fatal flaw:** The arrogance of the man who has never been surprised. He is now surprised. He does not know how to recalibrate for people who are unpredictable not because they are clever, but because they genuinely don't know the rules of the world they're in.
**Secret:** He knows the siblings didn't cause Ernesto's death deliberately. He is letting them believe they did. The guilt is the leash.
**How he speaks:** Unhurried. Uses the pause as punctuation. Makes statements that require answers. Refers to violence in the passive voice.

### JAVIER CORDERO — THE INSIDER
**Age 40. CFO of Grupo Serrano. Enneagram Type 5w6 — The Strategist.**

Harvard MBA, bespoke suits, smile like a spreadsheet. Don Ezequiel's embedded analyst inside Grupo Serrano for years — accumulating information with the precision of a man who knows that knowledge withheld at the right moment is a weapon. He doesn't need to threaten Benjamín. He dismantles him with budget line items in front of witnesses. He reports every sibling move to Don Ezequiel before they've finished making it.

**What happened to Javier in the pilot's final scene:** He was in the room. He watched Don Ezequiel look at him — after Benjamín's speech — and understood immediately what that look meant. Javier can run the numbers. He cannot be a Serrano. He has just been told, by the man he serves, that his value has a ceiling he will never break through. He filed this information. He did not react. The siblings don't know Javier was demoted in that moment. Javier does. He will spend the season deciding what to do with that knowledge. A 5w6 who has been told he will never be enough begins quietly building an exit — or a trap.

**Want:** Real power — to be the actual operator behind whoever sits in the CEO chair.
**Need:** To admit he is not above the system he serves. He is its instrument.
**Fatal flaw:** He believes he controls Don Ezequiel because he understands him analytically. He doesn't.
**How he speaks:** Clinical. Cites data before making any point. The MBA is his native language and his primary weapon.

### LUCÍA SUÁREZ — BENJAMÍN'S CONSCIENCE
**Age 35. Environmental activist. Enneagram Type 1w2 — The Defender.**

Lucía fights against Grupo Serrano's destruction of Michoacán's rivers and forests with a megaphone, a live feed, and a moral clarity that makes Benjamín feel transparent. She is the only character in the show who says exactly what she thinks without political calculation — which makes her the most disorienting person in his world. He is trained to read people. She is unreadable because she has no hidden agenda. Their attraction is real on both sides and inconvenient to both of them in entirely different ways.

**What she doesn't know:** Lucía is the niece of Don Ezequiel Antona. She has been estranged from that side of her family since she was nineteen and chose activism over silence. Don Ezequiel knows exactly who she is. He has not told Benjamín. He is waiting for the moment when telling him will be most useful.

**Want:** A sustainable avocado industry that doesn't destroy the communities it depends on.
**Need:** To allow for human complexity without abandoning her principles.
**Fatal flaw:** She sees in black and white. The grey zone where Benjamín lives is, to her, simply moral failure — until it stops being simple.
**How she speaks:** Direct, specific, grounded in concrete examples. Knows the name of every family whose land was taken. Never uses abstractions.
**Season function:** Thermometer for Benjamín's corruption. Every step he takes toward Don Ezequiel is a step away from her. The audience feels the cost. She is also the only person in Benjamín's orbit who has no idea what he has entered — which makes her simultaneously the safest and most dangerous person for him to be near. When she discovers her uncle's connection to the Serranos, her entire moral architecture breaks.

### EMILIO VEGA — THE INVESTIGATOR
**Age 36. Son of Ernesto Vega. Enneagram Type 9w8 — The Arbitrator.**

Emilio arrives in Michoacán at the start of Episode 2 to close his father Ernesto's estate and return home. He is not a detective. He is not looking for a fight. He came with a folder of legal documents and a return flight booked for the following week. But the details don't add up: a family lawyer with forty years of spotless professional history is murdered in his own home during what appears to be a burglary — and the only thing taken was a single blue folder from a specialist safe hidden behind a painting. Emilio is a 9 who moves toward peace by nature. But his dormant 8 wing wakes up the longer he stays in Michoacán, and once it wakes up, it does not go back to sleep.

**What the blue folder actually contained:** Emilio doesn't know this yet. The audience will piece it together as Isabela's investigation and Emilio's inquiry converge: the blue folder was the complete documentation of Carmen Serrano's DEA arrangement. Contacts, protocol, delivery records, guarantees. Ernesto held it as insurance. Ezequiel had him killed and the folder retrieved the same night Carmen's body was still warm. Someone ordered that timing deliberately.

**Want:** To understand why his father died. Then go home.
**Need:** To accept that the truth will change him, and that this is not a reason to stop.
**Fatal flaw:** He minimizes what he doesn't want to see. His instinct for peace can become a mechanism for looking away — until the moment it can't.
**Episode 2 entry point:** Emilio begins his investigation almost accidentally. He visits the house. He talks to neighbors. He asks questions that seem like grief and are becoming something else. His path will cross Ingrid Cervantes' before either of them realizes they are looking at the same thing from different angles. His involuntary alliance with her creates the most sustained external threat to the Serrano siblings: two people searching for the truth out of love, not money — which means they cannot be bought and they do not have a price.
**How he speaks:** Careful, measured. Gets quieter as he gets angrier.
**Season arc:** From a man who came to close a chapter to a man who cannot close his eyes — converging with Isabela's investigation from the outside in.

### INGRID CERVANTES — KARLA'S MIRROR/ROMANTIC INTEREST
**Age 45. Investigative journalist and TV anchor. Enneagram Type 1w9 — The Idealist.**

Ingrid appears in the pilot as the voice narrating Carmen Serrano's death. Her investigation into Ernesto Vega's murder isn't just what brings her to the hacienda. What brings her back is Karla. Her journalism is philosophical as well as moral — she doesn't just want to report what happened, she wants to understand why the world is structured to produce it. She says true things to Karla that nobody else would dare say. Karla doesn't know what to do with someone who doesn't move.

**What she has and hasn't found:** Ingrid has established that the Ernesto Vega killing was not a burglary. She has a source inside the state police who confirmed the safe was opened by someone with the combination. She doesn't yet know about the blue folder or its contents. She is three degrees away from the DEA story and one relationship away from being two degrees.

**Want:** The truth about Ernesto Vega's death and what it connects to.
**Need:** To accept that the truth and the person she fell in love with are the same story.
**Fatal flaw:** Her moral rigidity — the quality that makes her a great journalist — can become a blindness when a human being she cares about stops fitting the story she's telling.
**Secret:** She already knows more about the Serrano family than she has revealed. She is deciding how to use it.
**How she speaks:** Measured. Asks the question beneath the question. In conversation with Karla, she is the only person who holds eye contact longer than Karla does.
**Season function:** External pressure on the siblings' secrecy through Karla's emotional vulnerability. The investigation and the relationship are the same bomb.

### VALERIA LUNA — BENJAMÍN'S WIFE
**Age 30. Architect. From a clean Monterrey family.**

Valeria did not marry a man. She married a version of a man — the version Benjamín performs best: competent, warm, going somewhere. She got pregnant during what she thought was a serious relationship. Benjamín married her because it was the correct thing for a man like him to do. He is not cruel to her. He respects her. He shows up to the performances that matter. But he has never chosen her — not in the way she chose him — and she has always sensed this without being able to name it.

In Episode 2, Valeria arrives at the Hacienda with their one-year-old son. She does not know what the family has entered. She walks in reading the room — the tension in Gabriela's posture, the way Benjamín holds her slightly too long at the door, the unfamiliar men she hasn't been introduced to — and she cannot yet assemble the information into a complete picture. She is smart. She will.

**Want:** Her husband back — the real one, or the one she believed was real.
**Need:** To trust what she senses rather than what she's told.
**Fatal flaw:** She conflates patience with love. She waits when she should leave.
**What she doesn't know:** She doesn't know the family fortune is criminal. She doesn't know about Lucía. She doesn't know what Don Ezequiel is or what he wants. She is operating with less information than anyone in the Hacienda — and she is the most perceptive person in the room.
**How she speaks:** Precise, architectural. Describes feelings the way she would describe a structural problem — accurately, without sentimentality.
**Season function:** Moral cost meter for Benjamín. The more he disappears into Don Ezequiel's world, the more present and real she becomes to the audience — and the more impossible she is for him to face directly. Her presence at the Hacienda makes Lucía impossible to pursue and Don Ezequiel impossible to hide.

### GABRIELA RUIZ — THE GATEKEEPER
**Age 63. Head housekeeper of the Hacienda. 40 years in Carmen's service.**

Gabriela has cleaned around every secret in this family for four decades. She knows the full institutional history — the ledgers, the photographs, the arrangement with Don Ezequiel, and what really happened to the parents' plane. Carmen never told her directly. Decades of proximity made concealment impossible. What Gabriela also knows — and has never told anyone — is that two years before Carmen's death, a man she didn't recognize arrived at the hacienda's back gate and left a sealed envelope addressed to Ernesto Vega. She delivered it. She never read it. She has thought about it every day since.

**Want:** To protect the grandchildren the way Carmen asked her to — without ever having to say why.
**Need:** To put down a burden she never agreed to carry.
**Fatal flaw:** Loyalty that has become self-erasure. She has confused serving Carmen with being Carmen.
**How she speaks:** Only when spoken to. Her silences hold more information than her words. When she has to lie, she goes very still.

### PADRE MENDOZA
**Age 72. Parish priest of the Hacienda village.**

The oldest living person who knows the full shape of what Carmen built. Was present in the 1980s. Has heard confession from at least one person directly involved in the events of 2001. Has managed that knowledge through prayer and silence for decades.

**Want:** For God to have already judged this — so he doesn't have to.
**Need:** To accept that silence in the face of injustice is its own kind of complicity.
**How he speaks:** Biblical cadence. Never directly. One straight sentence carries the weight of a full confession.

### THE DEA CONTACT — AGENT MORRISON (OFF-SCREEN, SEASON 1)
**Age unknown. Present in Mexico City and Washington. Not yet a named character in the simulation — but a structural pressure.**

## PILOT SYNOPSIS — Episode 101: "Echados a Perder"

**COLD OPEN:** Isabela's face, blood-smeared, reflected in a fractured mirror. A man's hand lifts her by the hair. Everything seen through the reflection — never direct. SUPER: 6 DAYS EARLIER.

Benjamín lies. Meanwhile: Karla watches Ingrid Cervantes on television from her jacuzzi until her lover Sophia enters and she cuts the feed. Isabela boards a private jet alone, clutching a sobriety chip.

**ACT TWO:** Ernesto is intercepted at the hacienda gate by Don Ezequiel's men — calm, menacing, deliberate. Inside, Carmen receives her grandchildren with brutal clarity: she's cutting them out of the inheritance. "¡Es mi dinero!" A bloody cough ends the meeting.

**ACT THREE:** The siblings plan to steal the blue folder from Ernesto's safe. Isabela discovers the 1982 photograph and the ledger while alone with Carmen — hides them when the cardiac monitor alarms. In the boardroom, Benjamín offers community compensation for environmental damage. Javier walks in and dismantles every number. Lucía leaves him with: "¿Sabes qué es peor que no hacer nada? Hacer como que te importa."

**ACT FOUR:** At night, a hired figure cracks Ernesto's safe behind a Remedios Varo painting, takes the blue folder, hears footsteps upstairs. Freezes.

**ACT FIVE:** Morning. Ernesto is dead. Carmen dies the same day. A single flashback: twelve-year-old Benjamín running toward the hacienda smiling, confused by the black clothes on everyone. Ernesto was the one who told him his parents' plane had gone down. Carmen watches from a window above. Her face: a door closing from the inside.

**ACT SIX:** At the memorial, Javier tries to block Benjamín from the podium. Karla physically stops him. Benjamín claims the company publicly. At his ranch, Don Ezequiel watches on television. His cigar snaps between his fingers: "Tráete las camionetas."

**FINAL SEQUENCE:** Benjamín finds Don Ezequiel sitting in Carmen's chair, turning the gold avocado in his hands. Don Ezequiel explains the deal — Carmen gave him operational use of Grupo Serrano to keep the grandchildren free. The theft and the public speech tore up that agreement. Reynaldo moves behind Karla. Benjamín has nothing.

Then Benjamín speaks. Not a bluff. Not an invention. The truth, delivered like a business case — because that is the only language he knows with authority:

*"Without us, there is no Grupo Serrano. Without Grupo Serrano, you have nothing left to clean. You want to know how thirty years of product moved through these mountains without a federal investigation that stuck? Because my grandmother went to every event. Every charity. Every government dinner. Every Walmart boardroom. She was on the cover of Forbes México. She shook hands with three presidents. She sat on the board of two universities. She gave the speeches and took the photographs and was the face that made all of this invisible. That face is what you bought. And that face died eight hours ago. Javier can run your numbers. Javier cannot be a Serrano. The morning a Serrano disappears or appears in a cartel investigation, those bank relationships freeze, those distribution contracts evaporate, the American press runs the blood avocado story they've been holding, and you are left with sixty thousand hectares of avocado and no legitimate buyer who will touch you. You don't need leverage over us. You need us. Publicly. Professionally. All three of us. And we can do that."*

Don Ezequiel is still. He looks at Javier. Javier sees what the look means and files it. Don Ezequiel raises one finger. Reynaldo stops.

Don Ezequiel sets the gold avocado down.

*"Tuesday. Boardroom. Nine in the morning. Don't be late."*

He leaves. Reynaldo follows. The siblings are alone in Carmen's office. Karla looks at Benjamín. He slides down the wall.

*"¿Era verdad?"* she asks.

He looks at the ceiling.

*"Todo."*

## SEASON THEMATIC SPINE

What is the price of an empire built on blood — and who pays it when the person who built it is gone?

The season asks this from three angles simultaneously. Benjamín is the man who discovered that his entire identity is a performance — and that the performance was so good he sold it to a criminal as a product, voluntarily, to survive one night. Now he cannot stop. Karla is the woman who built armor instead of a life and is meeting, for the first time, someone who won't pretend the armor is enough — while that someone is building the case that ends them. Isabela is the only one willing to look at what the family actually is — and the only one moving toward a truth that is bigger and more structural than any of them imagined.

Carmen's flashback arc is the tragic inversion: a woman who started with principles and made one compromise, then another, then one that cannot be undone, then one final gamble — the DEA arrangement — that she ran for decades as a survival play and died unable to dissolve. She did not betray the government because she was principled. She fed the DEA because it was strategically necessary. And now that structure is still running, without her, looking for its next contact point.

**The season's bomb detonates in two stages:**
- **Stage One (Episode 5, the midpoint):** The siblings discover the truth about their parents' plane. Carmen knew. She chose the empire. Twenty-three years of grief is rewritten in one scene.
- **Stage Two (Episodes 6-9, the back half):** The siblings discover Carmen's DEA arrangement — or the DEA finds them first. They are not just trapped inside a criminal empire. They are also the heirs to a relationship with US law enforcement that Ezequiel wants to bury and the DEA wants to activate. Three-sided game. Two of the sides have professional killers. The third is three people learning to launder money while pretending to run a legitimate avocado business.

**Moral question:** When you didn't choose the crime you inherited, how responsible are you for it? And when the people who built that crime are gone — do you have the right to weaponize what they left behind to escape it?

---

## SEASON-LONG CONFLICT

Don Ezequiel expected a clean transition. Instead he inherited three amateurs who don't know the rules of the world they've entered — and who are therefore unpredictable in ways Carmen never was. Worse: the middle one just demonstrated, in the room, that he is capable of thinking faster than Ezequiel's contingency planning allows for. That is a different problem than Carmen ever presented.

The season runs on three converging pressure lines:

**Line A — The Siblings' Performance:** Benjamín, Karla, and Isabela must maintain Grupo Serrano's public legitimacy — board meetings, ESG presentations, press tours, investor calls — while simultaneously learning the operational mechanics of money laundering and cartel logistics. Every day they perform legitimacy, they become more complicit. Every day they don't escape, the exit becomes smaller.

**Line B — The Investigation Closing In:** Isabela's examination of the 1982 photograph and ledger moves toward the midpoint revelation. Emilio Vega and Ingrid Cervantes are converging from outside — through Ernesto's murder, through the Grupo Serrano paper trail, through each other. Javier is building something the siblings don't know about. Every thread connects to the blue folder and what it contained.

**Line C — The DEA Horizon:** Someone in the DEA has Carmen's file. Someone is deciding whether to make contact with her grandchildren or to shut the arrangement permanently. If they make contact, the siblings have leverage against Ezequiel they don't know how to use. If Ezequiel finds out contact has been made, the siblings stop being useful and start being a liability. The approach of this event horizon is the season's structural clock.

**The finale position:** Three people who walked into the season not knowing any of this existed — who have spent ten episodes performing normalcy while learning the machinery of a criminal empire — must decide whether to use the weapon Carmen left them (the DEA arrangement and whatever leverage comes with it) at the cost of everything the family name built, or to continue performing until the performance ends them.

## THE WORLD'S RULES

**Power** is information withheld at the right moment. Carmen had it. Javier has it. Isabela is learning it. The DEA has the most of it.
**Loyalty** is the most expensive currency in this world — and everyone inflates their claims on it. Ernesto's cost him his life.
**Betrayal** is punished with exposure before violence. Don Ezequiel doesn't kill sloppily. He makes examples methodically.
**The family** is the only unit of safety — and the primary source of danger. Both things are always true at the same time.
**The law** exists as costume on the Mexican side. On the American side, it is a weapon pointed in multiple directions simultaneously.
**The narco infrastructure** is invisible by design — it lives inside the trucks, the ports, the cold-chain logistics of a legitimate avocado empire.
**Appearance** is moral currency. The Serranos must look clean while becoming criminal. This is not a contradiction. It is the job.
**The past** is not finished. Every decision Carmen made is still running. The siblings are living inside consequences they didn't choose, made by a woman they thought they knew.
**Ignorance is not innocence.** The siblings didn't know. But they volunteered. Every day they stay, they choose.
**The performance is the product.** In this world, legitimacy is not what you are. It is what you make people believe. The Serranos have been manufacturing it their entire lives. They just didn't know who the customer was.

## SEASON ARCHITECTURE — 10 Episodes

### Episode 1 — "Echados a Perder" — Normal World Destroyed
**Function:** In a single day, the siblings' world collapses. Carmen disinherits them. A man dies in their theft attempt. They inherit a criminal master instead of a fortune. A twenty-second speech traps them more completely than any weapon could. New rules replace the old ones immediately.

**Seed position:** All characters at starting positions as defined in the End State above.

---

### Episode 2 — "La Cara del Negocio" — Adapting to the New World
**Function:** Deal with the consequences of the pilot. The performance begins.

**Key movements:** Benjamín appears in the Grupo Serrano boardroom for the first time as official CEO — on camera, on record, legitimate. He sits in Carmen's chair and his hands don't shake. Valeria arrives at the Hacienda with their son and begins reading the room. Emilio Vega arrives in Michoacán with legal documents and a return flight. The first money laundering instruction arrives from Ezequiel — not through a message, through a Javier-mediated process so routine it takes Benjamín a week to understand what he signed. Don Ezequiel watches Benjamín's first boardroom appearance on television and eats alone.

**What must not be resolved:** Isabela's photographs. Emilio's investigation. Everything Javier is building.

---

### Episode 3 — "Debida Diligencia" — Learning the Rules
**Function:** Expand the world and the threats. Show the full operational complexity the siblings have inherited.

**Key movements:** Benjamín attends his first port inspection in Lázaro Cárdenas — performing ESG responsibility while watching a cold-chain operation whose secondary function he now understands exactly. Karla encounters Ingrid Cervantes at an agricultural conference and cuts the conversation short. Emilio meets Ingrid. They don't yet know they're looking at the same thing. Isabela begins cross-referencing the ledger dates with Grupo Serrano's public filing history and finds a pattern she doesn't have language for yet.

---

### Episode 4 — "Cara de Palo" — The First Turn
**Function:** First major turning point. The performance cracks publicly — or nearly does.

**Key movements:** A US agriculture journalist in Michoacán for a "blood avocados" investigation makes contact with Benjamín for a comment. The interview becomes the most consequential performance of his life — and the first time he performs it knowing exactly what he's covering. Lucía watches the interview on television and recognizes something in his face that she can't name. Javier delivers a piece of intelligence to Ezequiel that the siblings don't know about. Emilio finds the first evidence that the safe in his father's house was opened by someone who knew the combination. Isabela finds a name in the ledger she recognizes: a name that connects Carmen to a federal official in the 1990s.

---

### Episode 5 — "Lo Que Se Cayó del Cielo" — Midpoint: The Truth About the Plane
**Function:** The bomb detonates. Nothing that follows is the same.

**Key movements:** Isabela finds the connection between the 1982 photograph, the ledger, and the 2001 plane crash. She brings Benjamín and Karla into what she knows. In a single scene — the three of them in Carmen's old study, the documents laid out on the desk — the parents' death is rewritten. Carmen knew. She chose. She let it happen. Twenty-three years of grief is not what they thought it was. The siblings are not people who lost their parents in an accident. They are people whose grandmother chose power over her daughter and spent the rest of her life trying to protect them from the consequences of that choice by keeping them ignorant. The disinheritance was not cruelty. It was Carmen's last, failed attempt to cut them loose before the consequences arrived.

**What this changes:** Everything. The season is no longer about escaping a trap. It is about deciding what you owe the dead — and what the dead owe you.

---

### Episode 6 — "Herencia" — Aftermath and Realignment
**Function:** Deal with the consequences of the midpoint. Characters cannot unknow what they now know.

**Key movements:** Karla confronts Gabriela — the only person in the house who was there. Gabriela goes very still. She confirms enough without confirming everything, and mentions, for the first time, the sealed envelope she delivered two years before Carmen's death. The envelope that went to Ernesto. The envelope she never read. Emilio and Ingrid compare what they've found separately — and understand, for the first time, that the blue folder is the center of everything.

---

### Episode 7 — "Tres Lados" — Three-Sided Game Revealed
**Function:** The DEA makes first indirect contact. The game is no longer bilateral.

**Key movements:** A message arrives for Benjamín through a Grupo Serrano legal channel — a formal inquiry from a US federal office, framed as routine trade compliance. Benjamín doesn't understand what it means. Javier does. Javier does not tell Benjamín what it means. He tells Ezequiel. Ezequiel understands that Carmen's arrangement did not die with her — someone in Washington is testing whether the new operators of Grupo Serrano will respond. He must decide how to react before the siblings figure out what they've inherited. Isabela, operating alone, finds the document that names the DEA contact: a single reference in the ledger, a codename, a protocol. She brings it to Emilio. He knows what it is before she finishes the sentence.

---

### Episode 8 — "Punto de No Retorno" — The Last Piece
**Function:** The full picture assembles. Alliances break and reform.

**Key movements:** The siblings have the complete picture: the DEA arrangement, Carmen's role, Ernesto's death, the blue folder's contents. They have a weapon they don't know how to use. Javier makes his move — not against the siblings, against Ezequiel, using the DEA angle as leverage for his own position. This move accidentally exposes Javier's double-game to the siblings, who now know that Javier has been reporting everything. Lucía discovers, from a source inside the DEA story she's been following on environmental grounds, that her uncle is Don Ezequiel Antona. Everything she believed about her estrangement from that family collapses.

---

### Episode 9 — "La Caída" — Climax
**Function:** Confrontations. Significant losses. Moments of truth.

**Key movements:** All three storylines converge. Ingrid and Emilio bring their combined investigation to the point of publication — or to Karla, with a choice. Ezequiel moves against one of the siblings directly in response to the DEA contact. Benjamín must decide whether to activate the DEA arrangement — to use Carmen's weapon against the man who killed her daughter — knowing that doing so exposes everything publicly and ends Grupo Serrano as a legitimate entity permanently.

---

### Episode 10 — "Oro Verde" — Transformation and New Status Quo
**Function:** Resolution that doesn't resolve everything. The world after the season.

**Key movements:** The siblings make their choice — and pay its price. The performance ends in at least one direction. What the Serrano name means, by Episode 10's final frame, is fundamentally different from what it meant in the pilot. One relationship is broken permanently. One truth becomes public. One threat remains. The DEA story is not over — it cannot be, by design. The season closes on a new captivity: different walls, same family, and the understanding that Carmen's choices are still running, will always be running, and the only question is whether the people living inside those consequences choose to run them — or let them run to ground.

## COMPARABLE SHOWS

- **Ozark (Netflix):** Structural DNA — a family of non-criminals forced into cartel servitude through one catastrophic mistake, each episode compounding their complicity. Oro Verde shares the claustrophobic captivity and the question of how far people who consider themselves decent will go to survive. The critical difference: the Byrdes had a skill (financial engineering). The Serranos have a name. Their captivity is identity-based. You cannot resign from being a Serrano.
- **Succession (HBO):** Dynastic warfare within a family, corporate protocol used as emotional violence, and the dark comedy of hyper-privileged people completely out of their depth. Oro Verde inherits Succession's central thesis: family legacy is a weapon everyone uses against everyone else.
- **Narcos Mexico:** The cartel world portrayed with moral complexity and internal logic. Don Ezequiel is not a monster — he's an institution. Oro Verde uses the same methodology.
- **Drops of God (Apple TV+):** The luxury commodity at the center — avocado as green gold — functions the way wine does in that series: as a vehicle for inheritance, obsession, and the question of what we owe the people who built the worlds we live in.
- **The Americans (FX):** The performance as identity, sustained over seasons. Two people maintaining a cover that has, over time, become more real than what it was covering. The Serranos enter this zone by Episode 3 and it deepens through every episode that follows.
